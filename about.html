Clam Gardens:

An Exploration of Shellfish Mariculture on the Northwest Coast of North America

This website is dedicated to understanding clam gardens by engaging the Lepofsky et al. article, “Shellfish Mariculture on the Northwest Coast of North America”, while considering other related research.

What is this website for?

This website is dedicated to understanding clam gardens by presenting the article “Shellfish Mariculture on the Northwest Coast of North America” by Lepofsky et al. (American Antiquity 2015), in addition to other related research. This website was created to satisfy a course requirement for an Archaeology of the Northwest Coast course (ANTH 449) taught by Dr. Quentin Mackie at the University of Victoria. Our goal is to take an example of dense and technical archaeological literature and make it both accessible and understandable to a public audience. Having previously learned about and interacted with clam gardens, our group was eager to work with the Lepofsky et al. article. We hope that our efforts create interest in both Northwest Coast archaeology and clam gardens in particular.

What is this article about?

Up until the mid 1990’s, management of marine ecosystems had largely been ignored by archaeologists working on the Northwest Coast. This article explores some of the first archaeological research, published in 2015, to consider the Northwest Coast maricultural practice of clam gardening. To do so, the researchers Dana Lepofsky, Nicole Smith, Nathan Cardinal, John Harper, Mary Morris, Gitla (Elroy White), Randy Bouchard, Dorothy Kenndey, Anne Saloman, Michelle Puckett, Kirsten Rowell and Eric McLay (here forward referred to as Lepofsky et al.) integrate Indigenous ecological knowledge, coastal geomorphological observations, and archaeological data to understand how clam garden cultivation practices maintained and increased clam productivity. As a part of this research project, the authors excavated clam gardens in four locations in British Columbia to better understand their ages, architecture, and construction, as well as their ecological and social importance. The information generated by this research highlights the extent and diversity of Indigenous relationships to the land, as well as the value of combining diverse sources of knowledge.

Why is this research important?

Clams and clam harvesting practices are economically, culturally, socially and spiritually important to Indigenous peoples on the Northwest Coast. Therefore, the research published by Lepofsky et al. is important because it acknowledges important and ancient clam traditions that have been previously overlooked by archaeologists and non-Indigenous peoples at large. It is important to make the knowledge generated by Lepofsky et al.’s research accessible to the public because it creates awareness that these structures and traditions exist while acknowledging the complexity and antiquity of Northwest Coast Indigenous people’s relationships to the land.
