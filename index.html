What is this article about?

Up until the mid 1990’s, management of marine ecosystems had largely been ignored by archaeologists working on the Northwest Coast. This website explores some of the first archaeological research, published in 2015, to consider the Northwest Coast maricultural practice of clam gardening. To do so, researchers Lepofsky, Smith, Cardinal, Harper, Morris, Gitla, Bouchard, Kenndey, Saloman, Puckett, Rowell and McLay (here forward referred to as Lepofsky et al.) integrate Indigenous ecological knowledge, coastal geomorphological observations, and archaeological data to understand how clam garden cultivation practices maintained and increased clam productivity. As a part of this research project, the authors excavated clam gardens in four locations in British Columbia to better understand their ages, architecture, construction, as well as their ecological and social importance. The information generated by this research highlights the extent and diversity of Indigenous relationships to the landscape, as well as the value of combining diverse sources of knowledge.

Why is it important?

Clams and clam harvesting practices are economically, culturally, socially and spiritually important to Indigenous peoples on the Northwest Coast. Therefore, the research published by Lepofsky et al. is important because it acknowledges an important and ancient tradition related to clams that has previously been overlooked by archaeologists and non-Indigenous peoples at large. It is important to make the knowledge generated by Lepofsky et al.’s research accessible to the public because it creates awareness that these structures and traditions exist while acknowledging the complexity and antiquity of Indigenous people’s relationships to the land on the Northwest Coast.
